Some of the key contributors to Interlisp are no longer with us. This page is to honor and appreciate them for the contributions they made.

  <div style="float: left; margin: 0 1rem 0.5rem 0;">

  Warren Teitelman revolutionized interactive computing by transforming Interlisp into the first modern Integrated Development Environment (IDE).

  <div style="clear: both;"></div>

  **Contributions:**

  Warren Teitelman was a central figure in the evolution of Interlisp, fundamentally shifting the focus of software development from machine efficiency to programmer productivity. His work began at BBN (Bolt, Beranek and Newman), where he developed BBN-LISP and introduced the "Programmer's Assistant," a suite of tools that included the first-ever Undo/Redo function and the DWIM (Do What I Mean) facility to automatically correct typographical errors. Upon moving to Xerox PARC in 1973, Teitelman helped rename the system to Interlisp and expanded it into a comprehensive Integrated Development Environment (IDE). At PARC, he pioneered advanced features such as Masterscope (a tool for cross-referencing and analyzing large codebases), the CLISP conversational interface, and D-Lisp, one of the first client-server windowing systems. His philosophy, often called the Teitelman Principle, emphasized that the environment should actively assist the user, a legacy that earned him the 1992 ACM Software System Award and laid the groundwork for modern interactive programming.

  Danny Bobrow was a fundamental architect of the Interlisp environment, overseeing its evolution from its origins as BBN-LISP to a premier AI development platform at Xerox PARC.

  <div style="clear: both;"></div>

  **Contributions:**

  Beginning in 1966 at BBN, Bobrow co-implemented Lisp for the PDP-1 and later the SDS 940, where he pioneered the use of "two-level storage" and paging systems to overcome small physical memory limits. In 1970, he co-designed the innovative "spaghetti stack" to handle complex control structures. Upon moving to Xerox PARC in 1973, he helped rebrand the system as Interlisp, fostering a highly collaborative environment that integrated radical tools like the DWIM (Do-What-I-Mean) error corrector and the Masterscope program analyzer. His leadership at PARC expanded the system's capabilities into object-oriented programming with LOOPS and the Metaobject Protocol, work that eventually earned him and his team the 1992 ACM Software System Award for creating a pioneering integrated development environment (IDE).

  John Sybalsky was a pivotal engineer in the transition of Interlisp from a research project at Xerox PARC to a commercially viable product, playing a lead role in the development of Interlisp-D and the Medley environment.
  
  <div style="clear: both;"></div>

  **Contributions**

  At PARC and its subsidiary Xerox Artificial Intelligence Systems (XAIS), John contributed to the core system's evolution, specifically working on the integration of Common Lisp compatibility (Xerox Common Lisp) and the development of the Maiko virtual machine. When Xerox began to divest from its AI workstation business in the late 1980s, Sybalsky co-founded Envos and subsequently founded Venue, where he acquired the rights to Interlisp and continued its development for 15 years, porting the environment to modern Unix and PC platforms. His work ensured the survival of historically significant tools like NoteCards and LOOPS into the 1990s, and his legacy continues today through the Medley Interlisp Project, which uses his original Maiko implementation as the foundation for modernizing the system on contemporary operating systems.

  Steve Purcell significantly advanced Interlisp-D at Xerox PARC by developing the bitmapped graphics and interactive animation primitives that transitioned the environment from a text-based system into a high-performance, windowed visual workspace.

  <div style="clear: both;"></div>


  **Contributions:**

  As a member of the research staff at Xerox PARC, Steve Purcell made significant contributions to the intersection of computer graphics and the Interlisp-D environment, specifically through the development of interactive display facilities for the Alto and subsequent workstations. Working alongside the Smalltalk and Interlisp groups, Purcell authored pioneering animation programs and contributed to the implementation of the interactive graphics kernel that allowed Interlisp to transition from a text-heavy system to a high-bandwidth, window-based environment. His work was instrumental in integrating bitmapped graphics and local area network facilities into the Interlisp "virtual machine" specification, which enabled the system to support multiple overlapping windows, menu-driven interfaces, and real-time visual debugging tools. This graphical shift, summarized in the Interlisp Reference Manual, transformed the environment into a visual workspace where users could manipulate code and data structures through a direct-manipulation interface.
